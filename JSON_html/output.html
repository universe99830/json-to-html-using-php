 What We're Reading: Politics, Friendship, and the Search for Meaning + More!

Each weekday, we make available to all Trinity Forum Society members our brief curated list of reading recommendations in the areas of faith and spirituality, culture, the arts and humanities, politics, and social science. Below is a list of readings we think you’ll find of value.​
Politics, Friendship, and the Search for Meaning
SOURCE: Comment
BYLINE: David Corey

Too often, we hear of politics driving friendships apart. This is due in part, argues David Corey, to a contemporary understanding of politics as war, where victory is sought at all costs. Reflecting on the nature of both politics and friendship, Corey brings in perspectives from Aristotle to C.S. Lewis to encourage us to correctly order the two in our own lives.

See our Evening Conversation with Arthur Brooks, "Healing a Divided Culture."
Victorian Exvangelicals
SOURCE: Mere Orthodoxy
BYLINE: Moses Bratrud

As doubt and deconstruction continue to be discussed in evangelical circles, Moses Bratrud examines the thoughts and beliefs of 19th-century Victorian evangelicals -- who, as Bratrud argues, are perhaps "the first doubters to express unbelief in ways that are recognizable today." Exploring how the Victorians maintained faith through their doubt, Bratrud discusses responses to doubt and traces Christian apologetics since Darwin.
It will take not only policy change but also social change to turn the tide
SOURCE: The New York Times
BYLINE: Tish Harrison Warren

As gun-related tragedies continue to occur, Trinity Forum Senior Fellow Tish Harrison Warren explores how communities of faith can respond to this crisis, asking the question: "what does it mean to be peacemakers, to love our neighbors and to affirm the value of human life in this moment?"

An Antidote to Christian Celebrity
SOURCE: Plough
BYLINE: Andy Stanton-Henry

"In the spirit of the Jesuit principle agere contra (“to act against/contrary”), I’ve been pondering the practice of hiddenness as a kind of counter-virtue to celebrity." Andy Stanton-Henry argues that in a celebrity-obsessed culture, perhaps the most meaningful influence we can have for the kingdom is to live a quiet and faithful hidden life.

See our Online Conversation with Katelyn Beaty, "Celebrity Culture & The American Church."
